X.509 Certificate Serial Numbers

What they are, why they matter, and what breaks when they're weak.

Background

Every X.509 certificate has a serial number — a unique ID the CA assigns at issuance. It's used for revocation (CRL/OCSP), auditing, and preventing cert substitution. RFC 5280 says it must be a unique positive integer, up to 20 bytes. It says nothing about how to generate one. That gap is where vulnerabilities live.

The fix

Use a CSPRNG with at least 128 bits of entropy. In Python: secrets.token_bytes(20). The CA/Browser Forum mandates it. The 2008 MD5 rogue CA attack by Sotirov et al. exploited predictable serials — it wouldn't have worked with a proper CSPRNG.

Signature Algorithm Binding

RFC 5280 requires the signature algorithm to appear twice in every certificate — once inside the signed payload (tbsCertificate.signature) and once in the outer wrapper (Certificate.signatureAlgorithm). A verifier must reject the cert if they differ. Without this check, an attacker can substitute a weaker outer algorithm and reuse a hash collision computed for it.

The binding rule (RFC 5280 §4.1.1.2)

This redundancy is deliberate: because the outer field is not part of the signed payload, an attacker who can produce a collision for a weak algorithm could swap it after the fact. Requiring both fields to match — and verifying that match before trusting the signature — closes the gap. Modern TLS stacks (OpenSSL ≥ 1.0, BoringSSL, NSS) reject certs with mismatched fields at parse time.

Strict DER Encoding

X.509 certificates must be encoded in Distinguished Encoding Rules (DER) — a strict, canonical subset of BER (Basic Encoding Rules). DER guarantees exactly one valid byte sequence for any given data structure. Without this, two syntactically different byte strings can parse as the same certificate — a semantic collision that bypasses hash-based identity checks entirely.

Why DER matters for hash security

A certificate's fingerprint — used in CT logs, OCSP, pinning, and caching — is the hash of its DER-encoded bytes. If a parser accepts BER, an attacker can produce a second encoding of the same logical cert whose bytes differ from the original, yielding a different hash. This silently breaks revocation lookups, CT log matching, and HPKP pins without touching the cryptographic signature at all. Strict DER parsing at ingestion time is the complete fix.
